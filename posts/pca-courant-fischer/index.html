Deriving PCA from the Courant–Fischer Theorem

2021-09-06 ☾ A variational eigenvalue characterization of principle components

Principal Component Analysis (PCA) is one of my favorite algorithms because (1) it is foundational and broadly applicable, and (2) it can be derived using approaches from many different mathematical fields.[1]

[1] The second chapter of Vidal et al. (2015) presents PCA from three different perspectives: statistical, geometric, and rank-minimization.
Typically, these derivations involve optimizing a high-dimensional objective function with Lagrange multipliers. Instead, the Courant–Fischer Theorem, a fundamental theorem from linear algebra, can be used to more simply arrive at a solution to this optimization problem.[2]

[2] This post synthesizes ideas I encountered in Matrix Analysis (JHU.AMS.792) and Unsupervised Learning (JHU.BME.692).
In this post, we prove the Courant–Fischer Theorem, use it to derive PCA, and finally consider some technical points from the derivation.

Formulating PCA as a matrix optimization problem

One view of PCA can be motivated by the following question:

Given a set of high-dimensional data points, what are the directions of maximal variance?

Represent one such high-dimensional data point with a random vector x∈RDx \in \mathbb{R}^Dx∈RD and assume the following:

  • E[x]=0\mathbb{E}[x] = 0E[x]=0 (i.e., the vector is mean-centered), and

  • rank(Σx)≥d\mathrm{rank}(\Sigma_x) \geq drank(Σx​)≥d, where Σx\Sigma_xΣx​ is the covariance matrix of xxx and d≪Dd \ll Dd≪D is the dimensionality of our embedding.

These assumptions lead us to the following definition:

Definition 1 (Principal Components of xxx). The ddd principal components (PCs) of xxx are a set of mutually uncorrelated[3] random variables y⃗∈Rd\vec{y} \in \mathbb{R}^dy​∈Rd defined as

yi=⟨ui,x⟩∈R,ui∈RD and ∥ui∥2=1,i=1,…,d ,y_i = \langle u_i, x \rangle \in \mathbb{R},\quad u_i \in \mathbb{R}^D \text{ and } \lVert u_i \rVert_2 = 1,\quad i=1,\dots,d \,,yi​=⟨ui​,x⟩∈R,ui​∈RD and ∥ui​∥2​=1,i=1,…,d,

such that the variance of yiy_iyi​ is maximized subject to

Var(y1)≥⋯≥Var(yd)>0 .\mathrm{Var}(y_1) \geq \cdots \geq \mathrm{Var}(y_d) > 0 \,.Var(y1​)≥⋯≥Var(yd​)>0.

[3] That is, cov(yi,yj)=0\mathrm{cov}(y_i, y_j) = 0cov(yi​,yj​)=0 for all i≠ji \neq ji=j.
We can derive an important characterization of the PCs from this definition:

Lemma 1. Var(yi)=ui∗Σxui .\mathrm{Var}(y_i) = u_i^*\Sigma_x u_i \,.Var(yi​)=ui∗​Σx​ui​.
Proof: This follows from the definition of variance:

Var(yi)=E[yi2]−E[yi]2(Given E[x]=0.)=E[ui∗xui∗x](By definition of yi.)=E[ui∗xx∗ui](The dot product is commutative.)=ui∗E[xx∗]ui(Expectation is linear.)=ui∗Σxui .\begin{aligned} \mathrm{Var}(y_i) &= \mathbb{E}[y_i^2] - \cancel{\mathbb{E}[y_i]^2} &&\text{(Given $\mathbb{E}[x] = 0$.)} \\ &= \mathbb{E}[u_i^* x u_i^* x] &&\text{(By definition of $y_i$.)} \\ &= \mathbb{E}[u_i^* x x^* u_i] &&\text{(The dot product is commutative.)} \\ &= u_i^* \mathbb{E}[x x^*] u_i &&\text{(Expectation is linear.)} \\ &= u_i^*\Sigma_x u_i \,. \end{aligned}Var(yi​)​=E[yi2​]−E[yi​]2​=E[ui∗​xui∗​x]=E[ui∗​xx∗ui​]=ui∗​E[xx∗]ui​=ui∗​Σx​ui​.​​(Given E[x]=0.)(By definition of yi​.)(The dot product is commutative.)(Expectation is linear.)​

Therefore, we are looking for the vectors uiu_iui​ that maximize this quadratic form!

We need one final result before we can apply the Courant–Fischer Theorem:

Lemma 2. The covariance matrix of a random variable is Hermitian.[4]
Proof:

Σx∗=(E[xx∗]−E[x]E[x∗])∗=E[(xx∗)∗]−E[x∗]E[(x∗)∗]=Σx .\Sigma_x^* = (\mathbb{E}[xx^*] - \mathbb{E}[x]\mathbb{E}[x^*])^* = \mathbb{E}[(xx^*)^*] - \mathbb{E}[x^*]\mathbb{E}[(x^*)^*] = \Sigma_x \,.Σx∗​=(E[xx∗]−E[x]E[x∗])∗=E[(xx∗)∗]−E[x∗]E[(x∗)∗]=Σx​.
[4] A matrix AAA is Hermitian if A=A∗A = A^*A=A∗ (i.e., a more general form of symmetry that applies to complex matrices).

Proving the Courant–Fischer Theorem

The Courant–Fischer Theorem provides a very useful bound on all possible quadratic forms of a Hermtian matrix in a subspace using the eigenvalues of the matrix.

Theorem 1 (Courant–Fischer). Let A∈MnA \in M_nA∈Mn​ be a Hermitian matrix with eigenvalues λ1≥⋯≥λn\lambda_1 \geq \cdots \geq \lambda_nλ1​≥⋯≥λn​ and corresponding eigenvectors u1,…,unu_1, \dots, u_nu1​,…,un​. Then,

λk=min⁡V⊆CndimV=n−kmax⁡x∈V⊥x≠0⃗x∗Axx∗x . \lambda_k = \min_{\substack{\cal{V} \subseteq \mathbb{C}^n \\ \mathrm{dim}\cal{V} = n-k}} \max_{\substack{x \in \cal{V}^\perp \\ x \neq \vec{0}}} \frac{x^* A x}{x^* x} \,. λk​=V⊆CndimV=n−k​min​x∈V⊥x=0​max​x∗xx∗Ax​.

Proof: Because AAA is Hermitian, it is unitarily diagonalizable. Let U=span{u1,…,uk}\cal{U} = \mathrm{span}\{ u_1, \dots, u_k \}U=span{u1​,…,uk​}. Then, the intersection of U\cal UU and V⊥\cal V^\perpV⊥ has a dimension of at least 111.[5]

[5] From the Inclusion-Exclusion Principle:
dim⁡(U∩V⊥)=dim⁡U+dim⁡V⊥−dim⁡(U∪V⊥)=k+(n−k+1)−dim⁡(U∪V⊥)≥k+(n−k+1)−n=1 .\begin{aligned} \dim (\mathcal{U} \cap \mathcal{V^\perp}) &= \dim \cal U + \dim \cal V^\perp - \dim(\cal U \cup \cal V^\perp) \\ &= k + (n - k + 1) - \dim(\cal U \cup \cal V^\perp) \\ &\geq k + (n - k + 1) - n \\ &= 1 \,. \end{aligned}dim(U∩V⊥)​=dimU+dimV⊥−dim(U∪V⊥)=k+(n−k+1)−dim(U∪V⊥)≥k+(n−k+1)−n=1.​
Let w∈U∩V⊥w \in \cal U \cap \cal V^\perpw∈U∩V⊥ and express w=∑i=1kciui w = \sum_{i=1}^k c_i u_i w=∑i=1k​ci​ui​ (a linear combination of the first kkk eigenvectors). Then,

w∗Aww∗w=∑i=1kλici2∑i=1kci2≥λk∑i=1kci2∑i=1kci2=λk ,\begin{aligned} \frac{w^* A w}{w^* w} = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^k \lambda_i c_i^2}{\sum_{i=1}^k c_i^2} \geq \lambda_k \frac{\sum_{i=1}^k c_i^2}{\sum_{i=1}^k c_i^2} = \lambda_k \,, \end{aligned}w∗ww∗Aw​=∑i=1k​ci2​∑i=1k​λi​ci2​​≥λk​∑i=1k​ci2​∑i=1k​ci2​​=λk​,​

with equality if w=uk⇒V⊥=span{u1,…,uk−1} .w = u_k \Rightarrow \cal V^\perp = \mathrm{span}\{u_1, \dots, u_{k-1}\} \,.w=uk​⇒V⊥=span{u1​,…,uk−1​}.

Corollary 1. As a direct consequence of the Courant–Fischer Theorem, for any matrix AAA as in Theorem 1,

λk=max⁡x⊥{u1,…,uk−1}x∗Axx∗x . \lambda_k = \max_{x \perp \{u_1, \dots, u_{k-1}\}} \frac{x^* A x}{x^* x} \,. λk​=x⊥{u1​,…,uk−1​}max​x∗xx∗Ax​.

This vastly simplifies the constrained optimization problem presented in Lemma 1 – no need for Lagrange multipliers!

Deriving PCA

Theorem 2 (Principal Component Analysis). Given a random variable xxx with covariance matrix Σx∈Mn\Sigma_x \in M_nΣx​∈Mn​ with eigenvalues λ1≥⋯≥λn\lambda_1 \geq \cdots \geq \lambda_nλ1​≥⋯≥λn​ and associated normalized eigenvectors u1,…,unu_1, \dots, u_nu1​,…,un​, the iii-th principal component of xxx is

yi=⟨ui,x⟩ . y_i = \langle u_i, x \rangle \,.yi​=⟨ui​,x⟩.

Proof: From Lemma 1, we have that Var(yi)=ui∗Σxui with ∥ui∥2=1 \mathrm{Var}(y_i) = u_i^* \Sigma_x u_i \text{ with } \lVert u_i \rVert_2 = 1 Var(yi​)=ui∗​Σx​ui​ with ∥ui​∥2​=1. From the Courant–Fischer Theorem, we have that

max⁡u∈CnVar(y1)=max⁡u∈Cnu∗Σxuu∗u=λ1 . \max_{u \in \mathbb{C}^n} \mathrm{Var}(y_1) = \max_{u \in \mathbb{C}^n} \frac{u^* \Sigma_x u}{u^* u} = \lambda_1 \,.u∈Cnmax​Var(y1​)=u∈Cnmax​u∗uu∗Σx​u​=λ1​.

That is, the first principal component of xxx is in the direction of the eigenvector corresponding to the largest eigenvalue. Recall that, by definition, the principal components are mutually uncorrelated. This implies that, unless Σx\Sigma_xΣx​ is the zero matrix, λ1>0⇒u1⊥u2\lambda_1 > 0 \Rightarrow u_1 \perp u_2λ1​>0⇒u1​⊥u2​.[6]

Therefore,

max⁡u⊥{u1}Var(y2)=max⁡u⊥{u1}u∗Σxuu∗u=λ2 . \max_{u \perp \{u_1\}} \mathrm{Var}(y_2) = \max_{u \perp \{u_1\}} \frac{u^* \Sigma_x u}{u^* u} = \lambda_2 \,.u⊥{u1​}max​Var(y2​)=u⊥{u1​}max​u∗uu∗Σx​u​=λ2​.

By induction, we can show that the uncorrelatedness of the principal components implies the orthogonality of their underlying vectors, continuing to enable use of the Courant–Fischer Theorem. Thus, PCA has been derived!

[6] By the definition of uncorrelatedness,
0=Cov(y1,y2)=E[u1∗xu2∗x]=E[u1∗xx∗u2]=u1∗E[xx∗]u2=u1∗Σxu2=(Σxu1)∗u2=λ1u1∗u2 .\begin{aligned} 0 &= \mathrm{Cov}(y_1, y_2) \\ &= \mathbb{E}[u_1^* x u_2^* x] \\ &= \mathbb{E}[u_1^* x x^* u_2] \\ &= u_1^* \mathbb{E}[x x^*] u_2 \\ &= u_1^*\Sigma_x u_2 \\ &= (\Sigma_x u_1)^* u_2 \\ &= \lambda_1 u_1^*u_2 \,. \end{aligned}0​=Cov(y1​,y2​)=E[u1∗​xu2∗​x]=E[u1∗​xx∗u2​]=u1∗​E[xx∗]u2​=u1∗​Σx​u2​=(Σx​u1​)∗u2​=λ1​u1∗​u2​.​

Technical Considerations

What if λi\lambda_iλi​ is complex? Which eigenvalue is "largest" in this case?

It is true that there is no total ordering on the complex numbers (see here), so there would be no "largest" eigenvalue, as such... However, we have shown that Σx\Sigma_xΣx​ is Hermitian, so σ(Σx)⊆R .\sigma(\Sigma_x) \subseteq \mathbb{R}\,.σ(Σx​)⊆R.[7]

[7] This is because Hermitian matrices are unitary diagonalizable, and the diagonal matrix, which is comprised of the eigenvalues, is itself Hermitian.

Okay, but what if λi<0\lambda_i < 0λi​<0? That wouldn't be a valid variance.

Any covariance matrix Σx\Sigma_xΣx​ is positive semi-definite,[8] so we also know that the eigenvalues of Σx\Sigma_xΣx​ are non-negative, i.e., σ(Σx)⊆R≥0 .\sigma(\Sigma_x) \subseteq \mathbb{R}_{\geq 0}\,.σ(Σx​)⊆R≥0​.

[8] For any vector z≠0⃗z \neq \vec{0}z=0,
z∗Σxz=E[z∗xx∗z]−E[z∗x]E[x∗z]=E[(z∗x)2]−E[z∗x]2=V(z∗x)≥0 .\begin{aligned} z^* \Sigma_x z &= \mathbb{E}[z^* xx^* z] - \mathbb{E}[z^* x]\mathbb{E}[x^* z] \\ &= \mathbb{E}[(z^* x)^2] - \mathbb{E}[z^* x]^2 \\ &= \mathrm{V}(z^* x) \\ &\geq 0 \,. \end{aligned}z∗Σx​z​=E[z∗xx∗z]−E[z∗x]E[x∗z]=E[(z∗x)2]−E[z∗x]2=V(z∗x)≥0.​

What happens if λi=λj\lambda_i = \lambda_jλi​=λj​ for i≠ji \neq ji=j?

Recall that Σx\Sigma_xΣx​ is Hermitian, and therefore is unitarily diagonalizable. This implies that every eigenvalue of Σx\Sigma_xΣx​ has equal geometric and algebraic multiplicities.[9] Therefore, for repeated eigenvalues, we can pick any orthonormal eigenvectors from the eigenspace of λ=λi=λj\lambda = \lambda_i = \lambda_jλ=λi​=λj​, meaning the PCs are defined only up to a rotation.

[9] This follows from the Jordan Canonical Form!
